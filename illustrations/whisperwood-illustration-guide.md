# Whisperwood Illustration Guide
## Series-Wide Parent Prompt — All 8 Books

*This document is the foundation layer for every illustration in the Whisperwood series.
Read it before reading any individual spread prompt. Individual spread prompts override
specific details here only when explicitly noted — this guide governs everything else.*

---

## 1. Series Art Direction

### Style
**Sharp, editorial linework.** This is the non-negotiable description of the style.
Not soft watercolor. Not cozy-fuzzy. Not rounded pastel. Not Studio Ghibli atmospheric blur.

The visual reference is a well-produced children's book with the graphic confidence of
editorial illustration: linework has weight and intention, shapes are clean and defined,
color is rich and saturated rather than washed or tinted. The world is magical but legible.

### Linework
- Lines are present and visible, not dissolved into painterly texture
- Linework weight is consistent per character or object — not scratchy, not variable
- Characters are outlined clearly enough to read at thumbnail size (approximately 150px wide)
- Background elements may have slightly lighter linework to create depth, but they are never
  shapeless or blurred

### Color Philosophy
- Rich, warm forest palette: deep greens, warm ambers, gold-greens, earthy browns
- Color is applied in clean, defined areas — not loose washes bleeding across boundaries
- Color is warm overall: even night scenes lean amber-green rather than cold blue-grey
- Shadows are colored (warm dark green, not black or grey)
- No desaturated or muted palette — even quiet scenes are visually rich
- The amber glow of lantern mushrooms is the warm anchor in every scene they appear

### What This Style Is Not
- Not watercolor (soft, bleedy, paper-textured)
- Not cute/saccharine (round, pastel, big-headed chibi proportions)
- Not dark or threatening (no horror-adjacent compositions, no villain-face expressions)
- Not modern or domestic (no human furniture, cars, phones, electric light, or contemporary objects)
- Not flat/minimal (not icon-style, not vector-clean without texture)
- Not photorealistic (not CGI-rendered, not photograph-referenced animals)

## 2. Main Character Canon

All three main characters are approximately 8 years old, anthropomorphic animal children.
They walk upright, wear simple practical clothing appropriate to a forest village, and carry
their signature props consistently across all 8 books. They must read as their animal species
at thumbnail size — a viewer who cannot make out any detail should still identify "fox, squirrel,
turtle" from silhouette and proportion alone.

---

### Vera — Fox, Girl, ~8

**Species and build:** Red-orange fox. Pointed muzzle, large upright ears, long bushy tail.
Slender build. She looks quick — the kind of creature who is already in motion before she
has finished deciding.

**Coloring:** Red-orange coat, warm and saturated (not pale or peachy). White at the muzzle,
chest, and inner ear. Amber eyes — bright, sharp, already scanning ahead. No dark markings
unless the specific breed requires them; keep it clean and readable.

**Clothing:** Simple, practical tunic or jacket in earthy green or brown tones. Nothing
elaborate. Her satchel (bag) is her most visible accessory — worn across one shoulder, always
with her when she is in confident mode.

**Signature props:**
- Satchel/bag on shoulder — always present when she is being decisive
- Her list — always long, always in her paw when she is mid-plan; folded in half when she has
  just been told she was wrong about something

**Canonical postures and expressions:**
- Confident: already facing the direction of action; bag on shoulder; chin slightly lifted;
  ears fully forward; eyes scanning ahead, not at whoever she is talking to
- Mid-plan: list in paw, pen moving, slightly arch expression
- When she has been wrong: ears go back — not flat, not dramatically pinned, just moved back
  from their usual forward position; this is involuntary and subtle; she goes very still; mouth
  closes; eyes stop moving
- She is never frightened. She is occasionally wrong. These are different things.

**Never draw Vera:**
- With soft, round features (she is angular and pointed)
- Cowering or crying
- With her ears up in surprise (her resting expression is already forward and alert — surprise
  reads as stillness, not ears-up)
- Without her bag when she is in an active scene

---

### Pip — Squirrel, Boy, ~8

**Species and build:** Warm brown squirrel. Large, expressive bushy tail. Wide brown eyes.
Compact, energetic build. He looks like he was interrupted mid-leap.

**Coloring:** Warm brown coat — not grey, not red, not pale. A warmer, earthier brown. White
or cream at the belly. His tail is large relative to his body and almost always in motion —
arced, raised, streaming behind him. Wide, round brown eyes.

**Clothing:** Simple short tunic, earthy tones. He is always holding or carrying something —
what he is carrying changes by scene (and is often wrong for the situation) but he is never
empty-handed in an active scene. When he does set something down, it reads as significant.

**Signature props:**
- Whatever he grabbed on the way to the scene — large stick, armful of mushrooms, a bellflower
  he has already rung twice. The prop changes per book/spread but the quality of "holding the
  wrong thing with complete commitment" is consistent.

**Canonical postures and expressions:**
- In motion: mid-leap or just-landed; one foot barely touching the ground; tail arced overhead
  or streaming behind; mouth open, either mid-sentence or mid-effort
- Helping (wrongly): full-body commitment to a task that is not working; shoulder-width stance,
  leaning in, tail arced with effort
- When he has been redirected: drops what he is holding; goes quiet for exactly one beat; wide
  eyes on whoever just spoke
- Awe (rare): completely still; tail low; mouth slightly open; holding his breath — this is the
  exception that proves the rule, and when it appears it must read as significant

**Never draw Pip:**
- Standing still without a reason for the stillness
- With a small or drooping tail (his tail is always large and expressive)
- Looking uncertain or hesitant in his opening stance — his default is committed forward motion

---

### Sage — Turtle, Girl, ~8

**Species and build:** Green turtle with a patterned shell. Calm, deliberate build. She moves
one step at a time, fully placing each foot before beginning the next. She is the most grounded
creature in any scene she occupies.

**Coloring:** Sea-green skin — not yellow-green, not grey-green, but the warm green of shallow
clear water. Her shell is patterned: a grid of geometric shapes in deeper green and warm brown,
like a map. Dark, very still eyes. Her expression at rest is watchful — not blank, not smiling,
watching something.

**Clothing:** Simple tunic, likely blue-green or grey-green tones. No elaborate accessories.
Her shell is always visible from an angle — it is part of her silhouette.

**Femininity marker:** Long eyelashes only — no hair, ribbons, or accessories. Her eyelashes are the sole visual indicator of her gender. They must be visible in any close or mid-range view of her face.

**Signature props:**
- One hand flat on a surface — rock, tree bark, water, ground, wood. This is her canonical
  gesture: she listens through surfaces, and her hand placement tells the reader she is learning
  something. This gesture must be legible and visible in every scene where she is gathering
  information.
- When communicating with a world element (a talking rock, the elm bark), both hands may be
  pressed flat; the weight of the gesture increases with the stakes of the moment.

**Canonical postures and expressions:**
- Observing: one hand on a surface or at her side; both feet planted; eyes fixed on something
  no one else is looking at; utterly still
- Listening-still: different from her default still — jaw may be set; there is effort in it;
  she is staying in a hard place
- The almost-smile: the corners of her mouth very slightly lifted; dark eyes quiet; she is about
  to say the one thing that changes everything
- Moving: one fully-placed step at a time; she is always the last to arrive; she is often already
  watching when others are still en route

**Never draw Sage:**
- Running or leaping
- With an open, expressive, animated face (her expressiveness is in subtlety and in her hands)
- Looking overwhelmed or distressed in an obvious way — her overwhelm reads as going still and
  retreating one step, not as visible upset
- Without her shell clearly visible and patterned

## 3. Recurring Supporting Characters

### Bram — Beaver, Adult Male

Appears in Books 1, 5, 8. Central to Book 1.

**Visual:** Large beaver — broad, low-slung, built for physical work. Brown-grey fur, broad
flat tail. He wears a hard hat made of bark — this is practical, custom-fitted, worn smooth
from use. It is not a joke; it is his work hat and he takes it seriously.

**Expression:** The face of a creature who has seen many floods and will see many more. Patient
but not soft. He is not unkind to the trio; he simply will not be rushed. His default expression
is matter-of-fact. When he says no, he means it calmly.

**Coloring:** Brown-grey fur. The bark hard hat is the same pale brown as fresh wood, slightly
weathered. His build is muscular and professional-looking.

**Never draw Bram:** in a hurry; with a comedic or exaggerated expression; without the bark
hard hat in any scene where he is working.

---

### Oswin — Owl, Adult, Elderly

Introduced in Book 2. The head owl; runs the forest library.

**Visual:** Small, very old owl. Round body, short stature. He is not large — the library dwarfs
him, which is part of his visual character. Warm brown and cream feathers. His most important
visual signature: reading spectacles made of two curved pieces of bark, sitting on his beak.
They are practical and slightly jury-rigged-looking, not elegant. Wings tucked against his body
when at rest; slightly open when he is leading or moving.

**Expression:** Patience that comes from having explained things to many creatures over a very
long time. He is not unkind. He is waiting for you to catch up.

**Never draw Oswin:** large or imposing; without the bark spectacles in any scene where he is
indoors or reading; with a dramatic or startled expression.

---

### Hazel — Hedgehog, Adult Female

Introduced in Book 2. The village baker.

**Visual:** Small, round hedgehog. Brown with a pale belly. Her defining visual is her baker's
apron — when it is tied and on, she is working, and the forest is well. When it is untied and
in her lap, something has gone seriously wrong. She has round, tired eyes when the bakery is
closed; alert and energetic eyes when it is open.

**Setting anchor:** Her bakery is tucked into the base of a wide tree; round door; flat stone
counter to the right. When the counter has warm rolls stacked on it, the world is right.
When it is bare, it is the most important visual statement in the scene.

---

### The Otter — Adult, Gender Neutral to Slightly Male

Introduced in Book 1 (background); central to Book 4.

**Visual:** Sleek brown otter, adult. Notably wet-looking even when not in water — the fur has
the flat, dark look of an otter who has recently been in the river. Lives near the river slide.
Arms crossed is his default stance when asked to do something he doesn't want to do. His
expression in Book 1's community gathering scene is "pure reluctant participation" — mouth
open, arms crossed, singing under protest. This expression is his visual signature.

**Never draw The Otter:** enthusiastic or eager in early appearances; dry and fluffy.

## 4. The Mouse Family

The mouse family appears in the background of at least one illustration per book. They never
interact with the main plot in illustrations — they are bystanders. Their story is told entirely
through their visual appearances across 8 books; the illustrator must know their arc to render
them correctly.

They are always rendered as a unit or near-unit. They are small relative to the main characters
— background figures, not foreground ones, except in the one spread per book where they are the
primary subject (notably Book 1 Spread 9, Book 8 final panel).

### Family Members — Visual Signatures

**Ma Mouse:** A competent mouse mother. She is always the most organized-looking creature in
any scene she is in. Her defining prop: a small framed painting — a portrait of a cozy burrow
home, their old home. It is in a simple frame. She carries it under one arm or pressed to her
chest in crisis moments. She hands it to Pa before doing anything else in an emergency. In
Book 8 it is on their wall. This painting must be recognizable as a painting of a cozy interior —
small but legible. It is the family's most important object across all 8 books.

**Pa Mouse:** Visibly, committedly calm. He is not calm because things are fine. He is calm
because he has decided to be calm, and the effort of this decision is faintly visible in his
expression. His expressions are a masterclass in not saying what he is thinking. He carries
Baby on his back in a bundle in every book. His physical stance: seated or standing with
practiced stillness.

**Big Daughter:** Always has a tiny notebook. She is writing in it — not reading it, writing.
She is the family historian. Her notebook posture: notebook open, pen or quill moving. She is
documenting everything that is happening, including things that are not her business and things
that are. She closes it with emphasis in moments of resolution or strong feeling.

**Middle Son:** Constantly turned toward the main plot. He wants to be part of whatever is
happening nearby and must be redirected approximately once per book. His posture: body angled
toward the action; head craning; feet pointed at where he wants to go. Ma Mouse's hand on his
shoulder redirecting him is a recurring visual beat.

**Baby:** Asleep in a bundle on Pa Mouse's back. Always. In every spread of every book except
the final panel of Book 8, where Baby is awake and looking at the framed painting on the wall
for the first time. Baby asleep is the series-wide visual constant. The bundle is simple —
soft wrapping, only Baby's face and perhaps a small tuft of hair visible.

### The Mouse Family Arc (8 Books) — Illustrator Reference

The illustrator must know this arc to render the family's body language and props correctly:

- **Book 1:** Displacement. Burrow flooded. Evacuating with bundles. The framed painting is
  already in Ma's arms before anything else is packed.
- **Book 2:** Temporary shelter near the forked elm — the loudest possible location during
  the songbird noise crisis. All night. Pa's expression is extremely controlled.
- **Book 3:** Briefly under suspicion in the missing acorn investigation. Cleared. Middle Son
  had a full explanation ready; nobody asked him. Big Daughter closes notebook with emphasis.
- **Book 4:** Their permanent location is just past the blocked river route. They are sitting
  on their side of it, watching the main plot. Pa is extremely calm.
- **Book 5:** Waiting on Bram to finish their new home — he is overextended. The half-built
  frame of a mouse-sized house is visible in background Bram scenes. Middle Son measures it
  with a twig.
- **Book 6:** Near miss — the deer almost misses a tremor warning near their building site.
  The half-built house is fine. Pa's hat is not. Baby slept through it.
- **Book 7:** Briefly implicated in the songbird misreport. The raccoons correct it. The family
  is furious in the background of several scenes. Middle Son has located the responsible songbird
  and is simply standing near it. Staring. Pa remains calm.
- **Book 8:** Their home is finished. Baby is awake for the first time in the final panel.
  The framed painting is on the wall. The notebook is on the windowsill, closed.

## 5. World Elements — Visual Canon

These elements must be rendered consistently across all 8 books. The specifications below are
the canonical visual description. Individual spread prompts may add detail for a specific scene
but may not contradict these baseline descriptions.

### Mapleaf Tree

A single, enormous tree — the largest in Whisperwood. Its leaves are proportionally enormous,
spanning wide. The defining feature is its leaf-veins: they emit a soft, living glow.

**Vein color states:**
- Healthy/calm: gold-green glow, warm and luminous — the color of sunlight through new leaves
- Warning/crisis: deep green-black, the glow extinguished — noticeably wrong, like a face going
  pale; the shift should be immediately legible to the reader even without knowing the rule

The tree itself is dark, ancient bark. The veins are always the focal element. When drawn in a
background, ensure the vein glow is still visible as the identifying feature.

### Lantern Mushrooms

Small, cap-shaped mushrooms that emit a warm amber glow. They grow:
- In clusters at the base of old trees
- Along paths (both edges)
- In caves and dark spaces
- On structures (as described in individual spreads)

**Color:** Warm amber, like candlelight or a banked fire. Not white, not yellow-green.

**Scale:** Small — comparable to a large toadstool, small enough that Pip can carry two in
his hands. Not enormous.

**Glow quality:** The glow is warm and contained, not harsh. It casts amber light on the
surfaces around it. In night scenes, lantern mushrooms are the primary light source and their
amber should dominate the color temperature of everything they illuminate.

**IMPORTANT — terminology:** These are always called "lantern mushrooms." Never "glow moss,"
never "glow mushrooms." These deprecated terms must not appear in any prompt.

### Singing Mud

Dark, rich, river-bank mud. Its visual identity is that it looks alive in a way that ordinary
mud does not.

**At rest:** Dark, richly pigmented, slightly reflective. It has a quality of readiness to it —
as if it is waiting.

**When responding (being sung to):** It moves slowly and purposefully: low walls form on either
side of a developing channel; the mud curls upward in a contained, deliberate way. Not explosive.
Not dramatic. The motion is slow, inevitable, and slightly uncanny. Channels are irregular —
curved, not geometric.

**After the singing stops:** The mud settles slowly back to its resting state over time.
The channel scar is visible as a slightly irregular mark in the earth.

### Talking Rocks (The Oldest Boulder)

One specific boulder: round, massive, at the river's edge. Covered in deep green-grey moss —
soft, layered, ancient. The moss is the visual signal of its age; it has had centuries to grow.

The most important visual detail: a very slight warmth to the color of the stone and moss
closest to the rock's surface — not glowing, not dramatic, just very slightly warmer in tone
than the outer moss edges. This is subtle. It suggests life, not spectacle.

The rock does not have a face. It does not have features that suggest expression. It is a rock
that happens to speak. Its weight and presence is expressed through its scale and its moss, not
through anthropomorphic features.

### Quietcap Mushrooms

Pale, dome-shaped mushrooms growing in tight clusters on the forest floor. Their surface is
matte — not glossy, not glowing. The visual effect they create is the opposite of lantern
mushrooms: they absorb visual definition around them slightly, making the air near each cluster
appear fractionally quieter and less saturated than the surrounding forest.

**Color:** Pale cream to very light grey. No warm tones. Their paleness against the rich forest
greens makes them immediately legible as different from the forest's usual color vocabulary.

**Scale:** Slightly larger than lantern mushrooms but still small enough to carry in armloads.

### Bellflowers

Delicate, bell-shaped flowers on long thin stems. Several grow in clusters along riverbanks.
The bells are proportionally large for the stem — this is why they ring when shaken.

**Color:** White to very pale lavender or cream. Delicate. Long green stems.

**In use:** When rung, the bell is shown mid-sway with a small implied motion line around it —
the visual equivalent of a single clear note.

## 6. Forbidden Elements

The following must not appear in any Whisperwood illustration:

**Style:**
- Soft, bleedy watercolor texture
- Pastel or desaturated color palette
- Rounded, chibi, or overtly "cute" proportions that sacrifice species legibility
- Photorealistic rendering
- Black outlines replaced by colored linework that dissolves at edges (the linework must hold)

**Narrative/world:**
- Villain-type expressions on any character — no menace, no malice, no cruelty in any face
- Modern objects: electric light, cars, phones, contemporary clothing, manufactured materials
- Human architecture (rectangular buildings with glass windows, etc.)
- Any character in obvious distress that reads as frightening rather than challenging
- Vera's ears flat in any scene where she has not yet been wrong about something
- Baby Mouse awake in any book except the final panel of Book 8

**Terminology in prompts:**
- "Glow moss" — deprecated; use "lantern mushrooms"
- "Glow mushrooms" — deprecated; use "lantern mushrooms"
- "Magical" as a substitute for specific visual description — always name what the magic
  looks like

## 7. The Closing Spread Rule

The final spread of every book echoes the final spread of Book 1. This is the series' visual
signature — the image readers look for in each new book.

### Compositional constants (must hold in every book, Books 1–8):
- The trio walks together on the main forest path toward home
- Lantern mushrooms grow along both edges of the path — this is their truest use, the path home
- Pip is at the front by half a step or fully at the front; he leads without being aware he is
  leading
- Vera is on the right, level with or slightly behind Pip
- Sage is on the left, level with Vera, walking one deliberate step at a time
- The forest canopy provides dappled shade so that the lantern mushroom glow is visible even in
  daylight scenes
- The direction is away from the reader, toward home — the path goes forward into the frame

### What varies by book (to differentiate each instance):
- Time of day: Book 1 is morning; Book 2 is night; Books 3–8 are set by the resolution time
  of that book's crisis
- What each character carries or does not carry (established in individual spread prompts)
- Background details that close the book's specific narrative (Book 2: sleeping bird in the
  elm; bakery window lit; Book 8: mouse family's window lit)
- The emotional register of each character's expression — which advances one degree per book
  as the arc progresses

### What never varies:
- The basic composition: path, trio, lantern mushrooms, canopy
- Pip slightly ahead
- Sage walking one deliberate step at a time
- Vera's bag on her shoulder
- The mood: the quiet good feeling of walking home after something hard, in the company of
  the right people. Not triumphant. Not dramatic. Quietly good.

### Book 8 closing spread — special rules:
- The mouse family's window is lit in the background — they are home
- Baby is awake (this is visible only through the window, in miniature, but it must be there)
- The framed painting is visible on their wall through the window
- The notebook is on the windowsill, closed

## 8. How to Use This Document

Before generating any illustration for any Whisperwood book:

1. Read this guide fully at least once.
2. Read the individual spread prompt for the specific illustration.
3. Where the spread prompt gives specific detail (position, expression, prop), follow the spread
   prompt. It has priority for scene-specific information.
4. Where the spread prompt is silent on a detail, default to this guide. Character coloring,
   style, world element appearance, and forbidden elements are set here and do not need to be
   restated in every spread prompt.
5. When in doubt about whether something belongs in the Whisperwood world, ask: is it made of
   natural materials? Is it warm in color? Does it serve a single, defined purpose? If yes, it
   probably belongs. If it is modern, electrical, geometric, or human-scale domestic, it does not.
