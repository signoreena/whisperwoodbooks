## Context: Why the Original Budget Was Too High

The original plan called for $8/day per book ($16/day combined, ~$480/month). That figure came from a data point about $650/month producing 220–300 sales — roughly break-even on ads while building rank. It was honest about the math but did not flag that this is a cash-flow commitment most solo authors cannot sustain before seeing any return. At $100/month, the approach has to change, not just the numbers.

---

## Question 1: What is the most effective way to allocate $100/month in ads?

$100/month is $3.33/day. That is a real budget — it is not nothing — but it needs to be deployed narrowly.

**Recommended allocation: put all $100 into Amazon Sponsored Products.**

Do not split it across platforms at this budget level. Splitting $100 between Amazon and Pinterest gives you roughly $50 each, and $50/month on Pinterest Promoted Pins (~$1.67/day) is too thin to generate statistically meaningful click data. It will produce impressions but not actionable conversion data.

**How to structure the $100 on Amazon:**

Run one campaign only — not two. The original plan called for an automatic campaign ($3/day) plus a manual campaign ($5/day) running simultaneously. At $100/month total, running both means roughly $1.65/day per campaign, which is below the threshold where Amazon's algorithm delivers ads consistently.

Instead:

- **Weeks 1–3: Run one automatic targeting campaign at $3/day.** Automatic lets Amazon's algorithm find your audience. Your job is to watch the Search Term Report every 3–4 days and harvest converting keywords. This costs ~$63 for the first three weeks.
- **Weeks 4 onward: Shift to one manual campaign** using the 3–5 keywords the auto campaign actually converted on. Set bids at $0.25–$0.40. This is where your ~$37 remaining goes, and where you start to understand what works.

**Bid settings:** Use "Dynamic bids — down only." At low daily budgets, the "up and down" dynamic setting can spike your actual spend well above what you expect on a given day, exhausting your budget before the day is half over. "Down only" protects the budget floor.

**Keyword focus for the manual campaign:** Use 3–4 word long-tail phrases. "Children's books ages 4-6 animals" converts better at lower CPC than "children's books" alone, because the buyer's intent is already narrowed. Suggested starting keywords based on Whisperwood's positioning:

- "enchanted forest picture books"
- "read aloud books for kindergarten"
- "animal friends chapter books ages 5"
- "early reader series no villains"
- Comp ASIN targeting: Princess in Black, Elephant and Piggie (product targeting, not keyword)

**Expected CPC at this budget:** $0.20–$0.45 per click in the children's books category. At $3/day you are buying roughly 7–15 clicks per day. That is not a lot of clicks. It is enough to see patterns over 30 days, but you will need the full month of data before drawing conclusions.

## Question 2: Does the $100 constraint change which platform to prioritize first?

Yes — but it reinforces Amazon, not changes it.

At unlimited budget, the case for testing Pinterest ads alongside Amazon ads is reasonable because Pinterest has lower CPC ($0.10–$1.50 range) and longer content shelf life. At $100/month, the reason to stay on Amazon is simpler: **the person on Amazon is already in buying mode.** They are searching for a book to purchase right now. The person on Pinterest is in discovery mode — they may buy in two weeks, or two months, or save the pin and never come back.

For a new series with zero reviews, conversion rate matters more than reach. Amazon's intent signal is stronger.

**Pinterest Promoted Pins: defer to Month 3 or later.** By Month 3 you should have at least 10–15 reviews on Book 1, which materially improves conversion if someone does click through from Pinterest to Amazon. Promoting before reviews are in place means you are paying for traffic to a page that will not convert well.

**Facebook/Instagram Ads: still Week 6 minimum, same as original plan.** This does not change with the budget constraint — it was always contingent on having social proof. At $100/month total, Facebook ads are not in the picture at launch regardless.

**Platform priority order at $100/month:**
1. Amazon Sponsored Products — launch day (all $100/month here)
2. Pinterest Promoted Pins — Month 3, if Book 1 has 10+ reviews ($5–$10/day for a 7-day test)
3. Facebook/Instagram Ads — Month 6, only if organic traction exists

## Question 3: Is $100/month worth it, or is it too little?

Honest answer: it is worth it, but only if expectations are calibrated correctly.

**What $100/month can do:**
- Generate enough click data over 30 days to identify 3–5 keywords that convert
- Provide a modest ranking signal to Amazon's A10 algorithm, which helps organic discoverability
- Surface your book to buyers who are already searching in your category
- Cost you roughly $1.20–$1.64 per sale in ad spend (if your ebook is at $2.99 at 70% royalty), which means ads are close to break-even even at this small scale

**What $100/month cannot do:**
- Produce enough sales velocity on its own to move you into Amazon's bestseller lists
- Generate statistically clean A/B data on ad creative (you do not have enough volume)
- Replace reviews — a book with zero reviews will get clicks from ads but will not convert those clicks well regardless of budget
- Make up for a weak KDP listing — cover, description, and categories must be strong first

**The honest threshold:** Most self-publishing advisors put the minimum meaningful Amazon ads budget at $150–$300/month for new books. $100/month is below that range. It will generate data. It will not generate sales momentum by itself. The more important investment at this stage is the free ranking signals: KDP Select Free Days, ARC reviews, and organic traffic from Pinterest and Instagram.

**Verdict: Spend the $100, but treat it as a learning budget, not a sales driver.** The goal for months 1–2 is not to make money on ads — it is to find out which 3–5 keywords your buyers actually use, so that when you increase the budget (or when later books launch), you are bidding on proven terms.

## Question 4: What free or near-free activities should replace paid ads in the launch plan?

These activities directly affect Amazon rank and organic discoverability. They are not consolation prizes for not spending money — several of them outperform $100/month in ads for a brand-new series.

**1. KDP Select Free Days (free)**
The original plan already includes a 2-day Free Book promotion on Book 1 during launch week. This is the single highest-leverage launch activity for a new author. Free downloads count toward Amazon sales rank. A 48-hour free run can move a new book into the top 100 of its subcategory, which means organic visibility to real buyers immediately after. Do this on Day 3 of launch week, exactly as planned.

**2. ARC outreach to Bookstagrammers and librarians (free)**
Fifteen accounts contacted before launch. Even 4–5 reviews posted on or near launch day has a measurable effect on conversion rate. A book with 5 reviews at 4.5 stars converts ad clicks at roughly double the rate of a book with zero reviews. This is the highest-ROI pre-launch activity available.

Specifically for Whisperwood: children's librarians on Instagram are an underused channel. Librarians curate storytime lists actively, post recommendation content, and have credibility with the exact parent audience. Search #librarianstorytime, #storytimebooks, #kidlitlibrarian. Message 5 librarians with a free epub and a note about the "no villains, SEL-forward" angle — that language lands with librarians specifically.

**3. Pinterest organic (free)**
Three to five pins per week with keyword-rich descriptions is free and compounds over time. A well-optimized pin continues to surface in search results for months or years. At the $100/month budget level, organic Pinterest is more valuable than paid Pinterest at launch. Prioritize pinning from the website (using rich pins) so that pin metadata pulls in automatically.

**4. Facebook parent group participation (free)**
Three to four large Facebook groups: "Children's Book Recommendations," "Book Club for Kids," parenting groups. Participate genuinely for 1–2 weeks before mentioning your book. When you do mention it, frame it around the emotional intelligence angle, not the buy link. The community will follow the author account once they trust the recommendation.

**5. Goodreads author profile (free)**
Set up a Goodreads author account and list both books. Librarians and dedicated parent-readers check Goodreads. Ask ARC recipients to post their review to Goodreads as well as Amazon and Instagram. A Goodreads presence costs nothing and signals legitimacy to the audience that cares about it most.

**6. Email list with free incentive (free to $13/month)**
The "Whisperwood World Guide" PDF incentive from the original plan is genuinely strong. A one-page PDF with character descriptions, forest map, and world facts is a natural lead magnet for parents who are building a home library and want their child engaged with the world. Mailchimp free plan handles up to 500 contacts. This builds an asset — an audience you own — that paid ads never give you.

**7. Second book launch (near-free)**
Books 1 and 2 launching simultaneously is already in the plan and should stay. A reader who finishes Book 1 and immediately buys Book 2 doubles your per-reader revenue and sends two sales signals to Amazon's algorithm. This series read-through effect is worth more than most ad campaigns for a new author.

## Revised Priority Order for Paid Advertising

**Launch day:**
- Activate one Amazon Sponsored Products automatic campaign for each book. Combined daily cap: $3.33/day (split $1.67/book, or run one combined campaign if targeting the same keywords). This is the full $100/month budget.

**Weeks 1–3:**
- Watch the automatic campaign Search Term Report every 3–4 days. Flag any keyword that generated a click and a sale.
- Do not touch bids or keywords yet — let the automatic campaign find its footing.

**Week 4:**
- Pull Search Term Report. Identify top 5 converting keywords and 3–5 ASIN targets.
- Pause the automatic campaign (or reduce to $0.50/day as a "keep fishing" line).
- Launch one manual targeting campaign with the harvested keywords. Daily cap: $3/day. Bids: $0.25–$0.35.

**Month 3:**
- If Book 1 has 10+ reviews, allocate $30 of the $100 budget to a 7-day Pinterest Promoted Pins test targeting "best books for 4-year-olds" and "read aloud gifts for kids."
- Keep $70 on Amazon manual campaign, now optimized on proven keywords.

**Month 6 (if budget allows increase):**
- Target $150–$200/month total to move into the range where Amazon ads can drive meaningful velocity.
- By then you will have real keyword data, real reviews, and a series with multiple books — all of which improve ad conversion significantly.

## What This Looks Like on a Phone

Reena is managing this from her phone a lot. The Amazon Ads mobile workflow:
- The Amazon Ads app (iOS/Android) shows spend, clicks, and impressions per day. Check it once every 2–3 days, not daily — daily checking leads to premature pausing of campaigns that need more data.
- The Search Term Report is not available in the mobile app. Pull it from a desktop browser once per week (Seller Central > Advertising > Reports). This is a 5-minute task.
- Buffer mobile app handles all Instagram and Pinterest scheduling.
- The only task that requires a desktop is the weekly Search Term Report pull and any KDP listing edits.
