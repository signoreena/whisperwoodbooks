# Book 2: The Noisy Night — Illustration Prompts

*7 chapters · 9 illustration spreads + cover · Written for AI image generation*
*Style: Sharp editorial linework, rich forest greens and gold, warm painterly color, clean edges. All characters must read as their species at thumbnail size. Not soft watercolor — sharp, graphic, children's book editorial.*

*Mouse family arc in Book 2: Temporary shelter near the forked elm above the owl's library tree. Appears in 5 spreads minimum. Arc: settled in temporary nest beneath the loudest relay point → enduring the all-night noise → watching Sage work at the elm → watching the quietcap solution unfold below them → finally, first quiet night. Baby asleep in all spreads.*

## COVER ILLUSTRATION

**Type:** Cover image (portrait or square, full-bleed)

**Scene:** Sage alone at the loudest relay point in the forked elm, her hand flat against the pale bark, eyes closed, jaw set. The canopy above and around her is rendered in visual chaos — songbirds overlapping in mid-flight, their relay lines crossing and stacking, the sound visualized as layered curves of overlapping color filling the upper half of the image. Below the visual noise, Sage is absolute stillness. The contrast is the cover: the world is loud; she is not.

**Art direction notes:** The noise in the canopy should be rendered as shape and motion — overlapping arcs of warm yellow-amber and rust-orange, songbird silhouettes layered in ascending spirals, each one smaller and higher than the last. The color temperature above the elm fork: hot, agitated amber-gold. Below the fork, at Sage's level: cool green-grey bark, soft light. The visual break between the two zones happens at the elm fork — Sage's hand is placed exactly there. Her shell shows its patterned surface from the side. The bark under her hand should show a faint suggestion of warmth — the color where her palm rests is very slightly gold compared to the grey of the rest of the surface.

**Character position and expression:** Sage stands in the center of the frame at the fork where the elm divides. She faces the viewer at three-quarters angle. One hand is flat on the bark — the canonical Sage gesture, executed here with more weight than any previous spread because the stakes of placing it are visible in the visual chaos above her. Her jaw is set. Her dark eyes are closed. She is not wincing. She is not covering her ears. She is listening into the noise. Her shell is visible; her posture is planted and deliberate, one foot placed fully before the next. She is the most composed thing in the image.

**Mouse family placement:** Visible at the base of the elm, small, in the background. Pa is sitting very calmly with Baby asleep on his back. The framed painting leans against the base of the tree. They have been here all night. They are not looking up. Big Daughter has her notebook open. This is the cover — they are a background note, not a featured element.

**Mood/lighting:** Night, or deep dusk. The only warm light comes from the agitated canopy above and from a single cluster of lantern mushrooms at the elm's base. The contrast between the hot, noisy upper half and the cool, still lower half should read immediately at thumbnail size.

**One-line caption:** The whole forest is shouting; she is the only one listening.

**Full prompt:**
Cover illustration for a children's picture book. A young female sea-green turtle girl (Sage), about 8, stands at the fork of a large elm tree, one hand pressed flat against the pale bark, eyes closed, long eyelashes visible, jaw set, absolutely still. Her patterned shell is visible from the side. Above her the canopy erupts in visual chaos — dozens of small songbirds in overlapping mid-flight spirals, their relay paths rendered as layered arcs of warm amber and rust-orange filling the upper two-thirds of the image, each bird slightly smaller and higher than the last, stacked in ascending noise. Below the fork at Sage's level: cool grey-green bark, quiet light, a cluster of small amber lantern mushrooms glowing at the elm's roots. In the far background, tiny: a mouse father sitting with practiced calm, a sleeping baby bundled on his back, a small framed painting propped against the tree. Sage is the only still thing in an image of motion. Sharp editorial linework, rich color, clean edges, character legible as turtle at thumbnail size.

## Spread 1 — OPENING ESTABLISHING SPREAD

**Scene:** A Wednesday evening in Whisperwood — the songbird relay is already running, already layered, already a half-tone too loud. The forest looks ordinary at first glance but something is slightly wrong: the birds are too numerous, too overlapping, too present. In the hollow of the willow tree, Sage's window is lit from inside — the only warm window light in the image. Everyone else is asleep or walking home. Sage is not.

**Art direction notes:** The dominant color is the blue-green of evening forest — not full night, not dusk, but that in-between hour when the canopy has gone deep and the lantern mushrooms are doing real work. The songbirds are the visual disturbance: small, quick-moving figures, a dozen of them visible across the upper third of the image, each one in mid-relay — beak open, wing out, passing a note forward. They should feel like a system in motion, not a flock resting. The lit window of the willow hollow is warm amber against the cool blue-green — it draws the eye. The ordinary world is visible: other homes tucked into trees, a path, lantern mushrooms growing along it.

**Characters present:**
- Sage: visible through her window, or shown from the outside as a silhouette — her hands are flat against the interior wood of the walls. She is the only creature awake at this hour who is not a bird. Her face is not visible — the window shows her form pressed against the hollow's inner bark.
- Songbirds (throughout upper canopy): eight to twelve small birds visible across the spread, each in mid-relay. They are the problem, not yet named as such. They look industrious, not malicious.

**Setting:** The willow tree that contains Sage's home — a large old willow with a hollow partway up its trunk, a small lit window cut into the bark. Around it, the ordinary village of Whisperwood at rest: other tree-homes with dark windows, the path below lit by lantern mushrooms, the distant outline of the enormous Mapleaf tree at the edge of frame.

**Mouse family placement:** In the temporary shelter near the forked elm — visible in the mid-left background. **Action:** Songbirds relay through the canopy; Sage presses her hands against her walls and listens; the mouse family settles for what will be a loud night.

## Spread 2 — THE PROBLEM ARRIVES

**Scene:** Pip stands outside Hazel the hedgehog baker's door, early morning. The shutters are closed — unprecedented. Hazel sits on her step with her apron in her lap, untied, rings under her eyes. **Art direction notes:** The bakery is a warm, established place — it should look like somewhere everyone knows and comes to, currently closed, which is wrong. The shutters are the visual tell: they are always open in the morning. Hazel's apron untied and in her lap is the second tell — she is someone who does not sit still, sitting still. The light is early-morning cool rather than warm, because no baking has happened. The canopy above the bakery is not peaceful — birds crossing, relaying, small quick shapes in the background.

**Characters present:**
- Pip (center-left, just landed on the path): Crouched at the base of the step, looking up at Hazel. One foot still mid-step. His tail is raised. His expression: genuinely concerned, mouth slightly open. He has already pulled his bellflower from wherever he keeps it — it is visible in his free paw, not yet rung but ready.
- Hazel (center-right, on the step): A small hedgehog, brown and round, apron loose in her lap. She is sitting on the stone step of her bakery door, looking up at the canopy with tired eyes. Rings under her eyes — two small arcs, visible. She is not distressed in a dramatic way; she is simply exhausted, and the exhaustion is specific: she is too tired to be energetic about it.

**Setting:** The bakery exterior — a round-doored stall tucked into the base of a wide tree. A flat-stone counter visible to the right, empty. The stone counter is where the rolls usually sit; it is bare. The shutters are closed. A small carved sign above the door. Early morning light.

**World elements shown:**
- Bellflowers: Pip holds one stem, bell at the ready.
- Songbirds: visible in the canopy above the bakery — four or five in relay, crossing.
- The empty stone counter: no rolls. This is the clearest visual statement of the problem.

**Mouse family placement:** Not in this spread. (They are in their shelter; this is the bakery path.)

**Mood/lighting:** Early morning — cool pale light, no warmth yet. The lantern mushrooms along the nearby path are still glowing softly, the ambient light not yet bright enough to wash them out. The wrong-ness of the closed shutters and empty counter is the whole mood.

**Action:** Pip crouching at the step, bellflower in hand; Hazel sitting with untied apron, looking up at the canopy.

**Art direction notes:** This is Vera at maximum forward velocity — decisive, clean, slightly arch. Her ears are forward. Her bag is on her shoulder. The list is in her paw and she is not looking at it because she already knows what it says. The forest around her should be active with the relay — birds overhead, the relay noise implied in their numbers. Pip is running to keep up. This spread is about the confidence before the wall: she is completely certain she is right.

**Characters present:**
- Vera (foreground, center-left): Mid-stride, already facing the direction of the canopy. Her bag is on her shoulder, list in her right paw, ears fully forward. Her amber eyes are scanning ahead — not at Pip, not at the birds, at the path ahead. Her posture is completely decisive. She is slightly arch: there is a small lift to her chin. She is already halfway there.

His expression is caught between his usual enthusiasm and the first flicker of "wait, will this work?" He has not thought of a thing to carry, which is unusual for him. He is focused entirely on keeping up.

**Setting:** The main forest path toward the canopy — the Mapleaf tree visible in the far background (veins not dark — this is not a structural crisis, the Mapleaf gives Vera nothing useful here). Songbirds above, thick in the mid-canopy. Lantern mushrooms along the path's edge.

**Action:** Vera in full stride, already committed to her direction; Pip running to keep pace, slightly uncertain.

## Spread 4 — THE OWLS SAY NOT THAT WAY
**Chapter 4 · Full-bleed double-page spread, 3400×1700px**

**Scene:** Vera stands in the library tree before Oswin the owl — the interior of the library tree visible behind him, shelves rising into the dark, enormous in scale. She is very still. Her list is in her paw, folded in half. Oswin has just told her the loop cannot be fixed by adding more order to it. Vera's ears have gone back.

**Art direction notes:** The library tree interior is the visual star of this spread — overwhelming in scale, slightly vertiginous. Shelves rise in curves following the interior of the tree trunk, packed with scrolls and objects and record-boxes. There is enough ambient light from lantern mushrooms placed in sconces to make the space warm and legible but the upper reaches of the shelves disappear into shadow. Oswin is not large — he is a small, very old owl at the center of a very large room, and that contrast matters. Vera is smaller still. The room dwarfs both of them. This is the moment where Vera is out of her element: not because she is wrong to come here, but because the problem is not structured the way she assumed.

**Characters present:**
- Vera (center-left, foreground): Standing. Completely still. List folded in half in her paw — she has not put it away, she is holding it folded. Her ears are not dramatically flat; they are just back, the involuntary tell. Her mouth is closed. Her eyes have gone still. She is absorbing what she just heard. She has not retreated and she has not agreed yet — she is in the moment between.
- Oswin (center-right): An old owl — small, round, with reading spectacles made of two curved pieces of bark perched on his beak. His wings are folded against his body. He is looking at Vera with an expression of patience that comes from having explained many things to many creatures — he is not unkind, but he is not going to say it again. He is waiting for her to catch up.

**Setting:** The library tree interior — the great hollow of an enormous tree, its inner walls lined with curved shelves rising twenty feet or more. Scrolls, small sealed boxes, flat pressed objects between bark boards. Several lantern mushrooms in sconce-like holders at shelf height. The entrance at the far left is a shaft of natural light — the outside is visible as a bright rectangle. The scale of the library should feel slightly overwhelming even to the reader.

**World elements shown:**
- Lantern mushrooms: wall sconces — warm amber working light.
- The library shelves: world detail — this is the owls' domain, and it is encyclopedic.
- Oswin's bark spectacles: two curved pieces of bark, round lenses, sitting on his beak.

**Mouse family placement:** Not in this spread. (They are near the elm, and this is the library tree interior.)

**Mood/lighting:** Interior library light — warm from lantern mushroom sconces, cooler from the entrance behind. The overhead scale of the shelves makes the light feel contained and directional, not diffuse. Serious but not dark.

**Action:** Vera standing still with folded list, ears back; Oswin folding his wings, patient, waiting.

**Illustrator flag:** The folded list is Vera's most important physical tell in this book. It must read clearly: she had the list open and was adding to it; now she has folded it in half. She has not put it away. She is not ready to abandon it. She just cannot look at it right now.

**Style note:** Sharp editorial linework, rich forest colors, clean edges, anthropomorphic animals legible at thumbnail size.

**Full prompt:**

## Spread 6 — KEY IMAGE: SAGE AT THE FORKED ELM
**Chapter 6 · Full-bleed double-page spread, 3400×1700px — KEY IMAGE**

**Scene:** Sage stands at the highest and loudest relay point in the whole relay — the fork of the elm above the owl's library tree — her hand flat against the bark, jaw set, eyes closed. This is her hardest moment and her most important one. The canopy is chaos above her; she is not.

**Art direction notes:** The composition is designed around a single visual opposition: everything above the elm fork is motion and noise; Sage, at and below the fork, is absolute stillness. The upper two-thirds of the image shows the relay at its most overwhelming — birds stacked in ascending spirals, their relay chains rendered as visible overlapping arcs of warm amber and orange, three chains visible crossing at this exact point, one feeding the others. The lower third is the bark of the elm trunk below the fork, the lantern mushroom light at the base, the mouse family in their shelter, and Pip standing a half-step back — watching. Sage is the dividing line. Her hand is exactly at the fork where the chains cross. The bark under her palm should have the faint color-warmth noted in the cover illustration — the intersection point, where the sound is worst, where she is.

**Characters present:**
- Sage (center, at the fork): Standing at the point where the elm divides into two great branches. One hand flat against the bark at the join — the canonical Sage gesture, but with more physical weight here than any prior use. Her jaw is set. Her eyes are closed. Her shell is visible from the side. Both feet are planted. She has not retreated. The full cost of staying is visible in how hard her jaw is set, but she is staying. This is not peaceful — it is an act of will.
- Pip (lower-right, one step back): Standing at the base of the elm's upper branch section, watching Sage. He is not moving, which is significant. He is not speaking. His tail is low, his eyes wide, and his expression is something new for him: awe. He does not understand exactly what she is doing but he understands that it matters. He is holding his breath.

**Setting:** High in the forked elm, above the owl's library tree. The library tree is visible below and to the right — enormous, its canopy below them, which gives a sense of height. The elm fork is the junction point for three relay chains — this must be visually legible, with three groups of birds converging from different compass points (upper left, upper right, far upper center) and their relay lines crossing at this exact fork.

**World elements shown:**
- Songbirds: three relay chains, each from a different direction, converging at this elm — the stacking and crossing of the chains is the whole visual argument. Each chain is a group of four or five birds, rendered in a distinct arc. Where the arcs cross at the fork: the densest, most overlapping cluster.
- The forked elm: a large old elm tree, the fork clearly a major junction — the bark is smooth and pale at the fork where the branches split.
- Lantern mushrooms: a cluster at the base of the elm, warm amber in the cooler night-approaching light. They give the only ground-level warmth.

**Mouse family placement:** At the base of the elm, in their temporary shelter — and this is the spread where they are closest to the action, most foregrounded. They have been here all night; this is the scene the prose directly notes. Pa Mouse: sitting, deeply calm, Baby asleep on his back — he is not looking up, he is not looking at Sage. He is simply present, and his stillness echoes Sage's. Ma Mouse: the framed painting is in her hands, held to her chest rather than set down — she is holding it the way she holds it in a crisis moment. Big Daughter: notebook open, pen moving — she is writing this down. Middle Son: the only one looking directly up at Sage — his face is tipped up, transfixed. Ma has not redirected him yet; this is the moment just before she does.

**Mood/lighting:** Late afternoon approaching dusk — the light has gone cool and directional. The canopy above is lit with the warm agitated amber of the relay birds; the ground below is the cool green of the elm's shade. The lantern mushrooms at the base are the only warm ground-level light. This is the visual split the whole book has been building toward: warm noise above, cool stillness below, Sage at the line between them.

**Action:** Sage hand flat on bark, jaw set, eyes closed, staying; Pip watching in held-breath awe; mouse family at the base, Pa calm, Big Daughter writing, Middle Son looking up.

**Illustrator flag:** Three relay chains must visibly converge at the elm fork — this is not decorative, it is the mechanism of the solution. The reader should be able to see, looking at this image, why this is the crossing point and why standing here to feel it through the bark is how Sage knows. The visual argument must do the work the prose does.

**Style note:** Sharp editorial linework, rich forest colors, clean edges, anthropomorphic animals legible at thumbnail size.

## Spread 7 — THE QUIETCAP GROVE
**Chapter 7 · Right-page spread**

**Scene:** Oswin leads the group through the forest to the quietcap mushroom grove — Vera, Pip, Sage, two small apprentice owls, and the three raccoons who tried the eastern fork. The grove, when they reach it, is immediately, visually strange: pale, dome-shaped mushrooms in tight clusters, and the air around them feels different — quieter, slightly muffled, the visual texture of the world softer at their edges.

**Art direction notes:** The quietcap mushrooms are the visual star of this spread. Pale, dome-shaped, growing in tight clusters — their surface should be matte and slightly soft-edged compared to everything around them. The illustrator note from the book specifies they absorb light as well as sound: the air immediately around each cluster is very slightly less saturated than the rest of the image. It is subtle but perceptible — the mushrooms create visual quiet around themselves. Oswin leads the group from the front; Sage is at the back of the group, but she is the one who looks most relieved by this place — the quietcap grove is her respite.
