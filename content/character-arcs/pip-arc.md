## Arc through Book 1

**Book 1: The Rising River**

- **Jumps in:** Pip lands on Vera's head to deliver news she already knows. He arrives at the river "out of breath with a leaf stuck to his ear." He immediately finds a large stick and tries to help with it — no plan, just action.
- **Redirected:** Sage redirects him: "Come with me." He follows without protest.
- **Useful action:** He is the one who goes with Sage to the Talking Rock and waits — his willingness to simply be there and wait is quietly important.
- **Momentum is the right tool:** "Pip," said Vera. "I need you to get everyone who can sing." Pip's eyes went wide. He was already gone before she finished the sentence. His ability to mobilize the whole community is the thing that makes the mud-singing solution possible.
- **Warmth and emotional intelligence:** His response to Vera's admission of error — "You got us to the beavers. That counts." — is emotionally generous and pitch-perfect. He does not gloat or over-comfort. He just makes it better.
- **Final voice:** "We fixed it." / "I have a feeling it won't be the last." — he has the last word in Book 1, and it is warm and funny and right.
- **Arc status:** Consistent with bible. His impulsivity produces the stick-holding comedy but his momentum and warmth produce the solution. Pattern established cleanly.

## Arc through Book 2

**Book 2: The Noisy Night**

- **Jumps in:** Pip rings his bellflower the moment he finds Hazel unable to bake — immediate, no hesitation. He is already pulling out the bellflower before he has assessed what kind of problem this is.
- **Impulsive action:** He goes to the loudest relay point at the eastern fork and tries to out-shout the noise. It makes things worse: the birds relay his voice as a new message. "I think there is now a message going around the forest that says 'LOUD NOISE AT EASTERN FORK.'" He sets down his stick.
- **Arc progression from Book 1:** In Book 1, the stick was entirely useless but harmless. In Book 2, his wrong action generates something real: he located the epicenter, which becomes Sage's entry point. His impulsivity still misfires, but it now produces useful information. The series is correctly notching upward.
- **Self-awareness check:** "…I did, didn't I." — the ellipsis reads as surprised recognition, not pride. He genuinely hadn't connected his own action to a useful outcome until Sage named it. This lands as warm self-knowledge. Not smug.
- **Redirected with warmth:** Sage redirects, not Vera. She almost smiles. No mockery. Correct for this book and for this relationship.
- **Momentum as the right tool:** In the final chapters, Pip is the one who takes Sage to every relay point in the canopy — back into the noise he already made worse. He does this for her, without complaint, knowing it will be loud. His willingness to keep moving, even in the wrong environment, is what makes Sage's solution possible. This is his momentum in service of someone else's gift. Quiet, but correct.
- **Physical tells:** Drops the stick when Sage notes his failed plan — canonical. "Dropping onto the step beside her" — just-landed. Pulls out the bellflower immediately in Chapter 2 — prop in hand before thinking. All consistent with the bible.
- **Voice:** "Hm." / "Sage. Rolls." / "That is not helpful." — short, breathless, self-deprecating. Quintessential Pip voice throughout.
- **Arc status:** Consistent with bible. Correctly positioned as "Classic Pip, Books 1–2" — his momentum is partially right for the first time, which is the precise step the arc requires here.

**Minor issue flagged for Beatrice — Book 2, Chapter 7 (prose rhythm):**

Three consecutive Pip lines run without a break before Sage's closer:

> "Oh," he said softly. / "She's making rolls," Pip whispered. "Sage. Rolls." / "I'll be there first thing," said Pip. / "You always are," said Sage.

Pip addresses Sage ("Sage. Rolls.") but she doesn't respond before he announces his plan. Sage's reply "You always are" then functions as confirmation of a statement rather than a response to an overture — it loses a breath. The closer is earned; the setup crowds it. A single beat between the second and third Pip lines — a glance toward the bakery light, a pause, anything physical — would let Sage's reply land with more space.

**Proposed fix:** Between "Sage. Rolls." and "I'll be there first thing," add a short action beat such as: *Sage looked at the light in the bakery window.* This gives Pip's third line something to follow and lets "You always are" feel like a reply, not just an echo.
