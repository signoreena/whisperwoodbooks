## Arc through Book 1

**Book 1: The Rising River**

- **Hangs back:** Sage is first to notice the problem (she watches the water before anyone else), but she observes rather than acts — she rings the bellflower, which is the right tool.
- **Overlooked:** After Vera arrives and immediately declares a plan, Sage opens her mouth and then closes it. She is talked over.
- **Crucial detail:** She takes Pip to the Talking Rock. She presses her hand flat against it, closes her eyes, and waits. The rock speaks one word at a time — "A tree"; "It fell" — and Sage gives those words voice aloud, interpreting them for Pip as they come. The patience is hers. The connection between the words and the flood is hers. The rock supplies the memory; Sage supplies the understanding.
- **Overwhelm:** Shown in the singing mud scene — "At the back of the crowd, Sage took a slow breath. She took a step back from the noise. Then she looked at the mud… She took a step forward." Handled with dignity, not comedy. She knows her part and holds it.
- **Vindication:** Quiet. She says only "Bram will fix the tree tomorrow." Her observation was the turning point, but she does not claim it.
- **Arc status:** Consistent with bible. Her gift (deep listening, patience) is the key to the book's solution. Her overwhelm is present but managed. Pattern established cleanly.

## Arc through Book 2

**Book 2: The Noisy Night**

- **Hangs back / overwhelmed early:** Present and physical. Ch. 1 opens with Sage lying still in her bed, hands flat against the willow walls, feeling the vibration through the bark before anyone else names the problem. Ch. 5 shows her on her front step, unable to advance further into the forest — "Her head ached. Her shell felt tight." Both moments are rendered physically, not as internal monologue, and both are treated with full dignity. This is her hardest book, and the cost of her sensitivity is visible from the first chapter.
- **Overlooked:** Functional. Vera has a full list and a plan (scheduling the birds) before anyone asks Sage what she hears. Pip tries to out-shout the problem. Sage is simply absent from the planning — not dismissed by name, but her knowledge is not sought.
- **Crucial detail:** She visits all three relay points with Pip and, by keeping her hand on the bark at the loudest intersection, identifies that three relay chains cross at the forked elm and that dampening those intersections will break the loop. The observation is genuinely decisive and earned by her willingness to stay in the most painful noise when everyone else had already given up on physical presence as a diagnostic tool. The mechanism is satisfyingly specific.
- **Overwhelm treated with dignity:** Yes. The progression across the relay points (stops on the path, closes her eyes, jaw set) shows the cost escalating without being played for comedy or pathos. She stays anyway. The narrative does not explain that she is brave; it shows her staying.
- **Confidence at close:** Markedly higher than Book 1. When Vera asks "You're certain?" Sage says "Yes" — no hedge, no qualifier. She cuts off Vera's acknowledgement with "Let's place them" and leads the group to each spot without waiting for anyone's permission or endorsement. This is a clear and well-earned step forward from the quiet vindication of Book 1.
- **Arc advancement:** On track. She has moved from silent observer (Book 1) to active field leader of the solution sequence (Book 2). Her gift is named aloud by the narrative mechanism — Pip looks at his own paw and realizes he had not thought about the bark — without Sage having to claim it herself.
- **Issues flagged:** Two issues require routing. See review output for details.
- **Arc status:** Substantially consistent with bible. Confidence arc and overwhelm are handled well. Two issues found: mouse family prose exceeds the one-line rule (four sentences, not one), and the bible's specified solution elements (owl-brewed settling potion; Sage negotiating with songbirds for quieter shifts) are absent from the book.
