# Book 3: The Missing Mole
## A Whisperwood Story

*Resources: Underground tunnels · Mapleaf · Raccoons · Bunnies | Theme: Moving fast in the wrong direction takes you further away.*

---

### Chapter 1: Tapping Underfoot

It was an ordinary morning in Whisperwood.

If you stood very still and listened, you could hear the bunnies at work beneath the ground — a faint, steady tapping, like rain on a hollow log. They were always maintaining something: a new tunnel here, a shored-up passage there.

Young ones played near the tunnel entrances while their parents went about the day.

Vera sat nearby, adding to her list.

Pip was mid-leap between two branches when he spotted a small mole at the edge of the nearest tunnel entrance, peering in.

"That one looks very young," said Pip.

"New to tunneling," said Vera, without looking up. "Still learning."

Pip watched the small mole lean further into the dark.

He was about to say something. Then Sage set her hand on the tunnel entrance, listening to the tapping from below.

"They're working past the southern fork," she said. "Deep today."

The little mole tipped forward and disappeared inside.

---

### Chapter 2: Hazel at the Door

Hazel the hedgehog arrived before the trio had finished breakfast.

She was not calm. Hazel was always calm — she made rolls before dawn while the rest of Whisperwood slept. But today she had no apron, and her hands were twisted together, and she was already talking when she reached the door.

"Moss," she said. "My young cousin. He went in before breakfast — just to look, he said — and he hasn't come out. That was hours ago." She looked from Vera to Pip to Sage. "He's still learning the tunnels. He doesn't know them. He could be anywhere."

She had rung the bellflowers on her way — the raccoons would already be checking their records.

"Which entrance?" said Vera.

"The main one. By the oak roots."

Vera was already looking at her list. "How long ago exactly?"

Hazel pressed her hands together. "Three hours. Maybe a little more."

Pip's ears went up. He did not say anything. He was already thinking.

Vera had her bag on her shoulder before Hazel finished speaking.

---

### Chapter 3: The Eastern Branch

Vera checked the Mapleaf first.

Its veins glowed faintly, showing routes below the ground — heat signatures, movement, the warm press of living creatures through the soil. There, at the eastern branch of the main tunnel, a soft orange glow.

"Something warm in the eastern branch," she said. "Could be Moss." She pulled out her list and began drawing a search grid. "He's small. In three hours, a mole his size could reach—" She calculated. "The second or third junction. Eastern branch, grid squares four through seven."

She handed the grid to Pip.

"Go," she said. "Call his name. I'll work out where else he could have reached."

Pip had been holding a small marking stick — the kind the bunnies used to flag tunnel junctions. He dropped it when he grabbed the grid. He did not notice. He was already gone.

Vera began calculating.

Sage had not moved. She stood at the tunnel entrance, one hand pressed flat against the earth beside it.

---

### Chapter 4: The Wrong Branch

Pip ran the eastern tunnels for a long time.

He called Moss's name. His voice bounced off the packed earth walls and came back to him from six directions at once. He called again, louder. The echo grew into a many-layered version of itself.

There was no answering voice.

At the third junction, he found the end of the tunnels and a family of voles on their morning route, looking at him with wide, patient eyes.

"Sorry," said Pip.

He ran back.

Above ground, Sage still had her hand pressed to the earth.

"There's nothing in the east," said Pip, arriving out of breath. "Just voles."

Vera's ears went back. Just slightly. "The Mapleaf showed heat at the eastern fork."

"Voles," said Pip.

Vera was very still.

The Mapleaf had shown her something warm. She had not asked what something warm usually moved through the eastern branch in the mornings.

"I need to rethink," she said quietly.

---

### Chapter 5: Pip in the Dark

Pip was still standing at the tunnel entrance.

He looked down into the dark.

He looked at Vera's grid. He thought about what it felt like to be small and underground and not know the way.

He cupped his paws around his mouth and shouted down the entrance with everything he had.

"MOSS!"

The echo came back enormous. And then, somewhere in the far dark, it kept going — bouncing from junction to junction, east and south and deeper.

It was not Moss's voice coming back.

But it was not entirely nothing either.

"Pip," said Vera, pinching the bridge of her nose.

"I found the shape of the tunnels," said Pip, with some dignity. "You can hear where it goes."

Sage lifted her head.

---

### Chapter 6: Warm Air

Sage knelt at the tunnel entrance. She pressed both palms to the earth, then moved them to either side of the opening. She breathed in slowly.

The raccoons had arrived — Hazel had rung the bellflower before she came to find the trio, and they'd been reviewing their route records. "We have Moss going south," said the head raccoon, turning a page. "Small tracks, early morning, before the bunnies started their shift."

"South," said Vera.

"South," said Sage. She lifted her chin from the opening. "The air from the southern branch is warmer. Something has been resting in there for a while."

Vera looked at her grid. Then she folded it in half.

"Bonnie," she said.

Bonnie the bunny was already at the edge of the group. She was always nearby when the tunnels were involved.

"Southern branch," said Vera. "The side chambers. You know them?"

"Every one," said Bonnie.

---

### Chapter 7: A Nap in the Dark

Bonnie knew the southern branch the way Sage knew the surface of every stone she had ever touched: completely, from memory, without needing to look.

She led them without hesitating — left at the first junction, right where the root crossed the ceiling, then a short squeeze through a narrow passage and into a quieter stretch where the air was warm and still.

The raccoons had confirmed it. The bunnies had cleared the way.

A mouse family peered from a side tunnel as they passed — Pa steady and calm, Big Daughter's notebook already open, Middle Son craning his neck to see everything happening.

In the third side chamber off the main southern passage, Moss the mole was curled into a small, tight ball in a patch of dry soil. His breathing was slow and even.

He was fast asleep.

"Oh," said Pip.

Moss opened one eye. Then the other. He looked at six creatures staring at him in a tunnel.

"Is it breakfast?" he said.

They brought him home. Hazel made warm rolls. Vera added a new line to her list: *ask what usually uses the eastern branch before sending anyone east.*

Pip ran the whole way home and arrived first, which felt right.

"I should have asked Hazel which way she saw him go," said Vera, walking beside Sage. "That was the first question. I skipped it."

"Moving fast," said Sage, "in the wrong direction."

"Takes you further away," said Vera.

"Yes," said Sage.

---
*End of Book 3: The Missing Mole*

## About Whisperwood

Whisperwood is an enchanted forest where the enchantment is part of everyday life. Three friends — Vera the fox, Pip the squirrel, and Sage the turtle — solve problems the only way anything ever gets solved here: together, with the right creature, in the right order, one careful step at a time.

## The World of Whisperwood

**Underground Tunnels**
The bunny network runs below all of Whisperwood — passages, junctions, side chambers. The bunnies know every turn. They maintain the tunnels and guide others through when needed.
*Rule: If you don't know the tunnels, find someone who does.*

**The Mapleaf Tree**
A giant tree whose veins glow and shift to show routes and movement below ground as well as above. Reads like a living map. Can show heat signatures — but cannot tell you what is making them.
*Rule: It shows what is there. It does not tell you what to do.*

**Raccoons**
The journalists and fact-checkers of Whisperwood. They investigate, verify, and keep records. When the songbirds have the wrong version of events, the raccoons have the right one.
*Rule: They verify. They do not guess.*

**Bonnie (Head Bunny):** Knows every tunnel in Whisperwood. She finds her way without looking.
