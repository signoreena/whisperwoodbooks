# Book 7: Two Telling Truths
## A Whisperwood Story

*Resources: Songbirds (two versions) · Raccoons (third account) | Theme: You can both be right about what you saw and still be telling different stories.*

---

### Chapter 1: Two Sides of the Square

It was an ordinary morning, except that it wasn't.

Reed the bunny sat on one side of the village square. Wren the songbird perched on a branch on the other side. They were usually together — almost always together. Everyone knew Reed and Wren.

Today they were not together. Today they were not looking at each other.

The square was very quiet.

At the base of the big oak at the far end of the square, the mouse family's tunnel entrance was just visible between two roots. Middle Son was there, peering at something on the ground near the oak.

Vera noticed it when she came through on her morning route. She stopped. Her ears went forward.

Pip noticed it because Reed was always the one who told him when there were interesting things happening in the underground tunnels, and Wren was always the first to know what the songbirds had spotted, and they were never on opposite sides of anything.

Sage noticed it the way she noticed most things: she sat down near the fountain in the middle of the square, put her hand on the stone, and listened.

---

### Chapter 2: Two Versions

The songbird network was carrying two stories.

This was unusual. The network carried one story at a time — each bird relaying the last, on and on around the forest. Two stories running at once made the network confused and loud in a particular way, like two relay chains trying to use the same path.

The first story said: Reed had knocked Wren's nest materials into the mud by the big oak, and Wren had screamed at him, and Reed had walked away.

The second story said: Wren had startled Reed so badly that he'd dropped everything, and then Wren had said something sharp, and Reed had walked away without a word.

Half the forest was saying Reed was wrong. Half was saying Wren was wrong.

Nobody had asked either of them what had actually happened.

---

### Chapter 3: Vera's Logic

Vera had both stories written on her list, side by side.

She had verified the facts each story contained. Reed had been at the big oak at midday yesterday. True. Wren had been at the big oak at midday yesterday. True. Nest materials had ended up in the mud. True.

Both stories were internally consistent. Both contained verified facts. Both pointed to different conclusions.

Vera sat with her list for a long time.

"One of them is wrong," she said.

"Are you sure?" said Sage.

Vera looked at her. "They can't both be right. They contradict each other."

Sage did not say anything. She looked at the village square — at Reed on one side, Wren on the other.

Vera looked back at her list. Her system for finding the truth worked by finding the false thing. There was no false thing. She looked at the list. The list had nothing to cross out.

---

### Chapter 4: Pip Carries Messages

Pip went to Reed.

He listened. Reed explained exactly what had happened, clearly and honestly. Pip believed him.

Pip went to Wren.

He listened. Wren explained exactly what had happened, clearly and honestly. Pip believed her too.

He went back to Reed. He told him what Wren had said. Reed's ears went flat. "That's not how it was."

He went back to Wren. He told her what Reed had said. Her feathers puffed. "I know what I saw."

Pip went back and forth three more times. Each time, both creatures became more certain they were right. The village square got louder. Three new songbirds joined the relay carrying Pip's summaries.

There was now a third version of events. It was somehow worse than both of the first two.

Pip sat down in the middle of the square. Three songbirds circled above him, arguing.

---

### Chapter 5: What They Both Said

Sage had been listening to both versions all morning.

She had sat with her hand on the fountain stone and felt something she couldn't name — the same weight in both voices, pressing in from different directions. Like two sounds that came from the same place but couldn't hear each other.

She came and sat beside Vera.

"Reed said it happened by the big oak at midday," said Sage.

"Yes," said Vera.

"Wren said it happened by the big oak at midday."

"Yes."

"Reed said he was scared."

Vera looked up.

"Wren said she was scared."

A long pause.

"They were scared of different parts of the same thing," said Sage. "Reed was on the ground. Wren was on a branch above. They were both there. They both saw something. They saw different parts of it."

Vera was very still. "The big oak at midday," she said slowly. "From the ground and from the branch above."

"Neither of them could see what the other saw," said Sage.

---

### Chapter 6: The Third Account

The raccoons had been investigating the event itself — not the fight, but what had happened at the big oak.

They had questioned every creature who had been in the area. They had checked the mud for tracks, the branch for marks, the nest materials for where they had come from.

They had found the mouse family.

Their tunnel entrance came up right at the root of the big oak. They had been there. They had seen the whole thing from ground level — including the parts neither Reed nor Wren could have seen from where they stood.

The raccoon head knocked on their door and explained what he needed.

Big Daughter opened her notebook. She had already written it all down. She handed the notebook to the raccoons without a word. Middle Son looked deeply satisfied. Pa was, as always, very calm. Ma stood behind him with the painting, and Baby slept on as if nothing unusual had happened at all.

---

### Chapter 7: Same Oak, Different View

Pip went to Reed first. He did not carry a message. He just said: "Come to the big oak. There's something you need to see." Then he went to Wren and said the same thing. He did not explain. He did not argue. He just waited until they both came.

The raccoons walked them through what each of them could see from their position. Reed at the base: Wren landing suddenly above him, close, startling him, his materials slipping into the mud. Wren on the branch: Reed below, his motion sudden, scattering everything, and then saying nothing as he walked away.

One thing had happened. Two creatures had each seen half of it. Neither had been able to see the other's half.

Reed and Wren stood on opposite sides of the big oak and looked at each other.

"I didn't know I'd startled you," said Wren.

"I didn't know you thought I'd done it on purpose," said Reed.

The songbird network went quiet. Then it began to carry a different message.

Vera folded her list. "I looked for which story was true," she said. "I should have asked where they were both standing."

On the way home, the lantern mushrooms lit the path through the village. Sage walked between Vera and Pip.

"They were both right," said Pip.

"Yes," said Sage.

"That's a lot harder than one of them being wrong."

"Yes," said Sage.

---
*End of Book 7: Two Telling Truths*

## About Whisperwood

Whisperwood is an enchanted forest where the enchantment is part of everyday life. Three friends — Vera the fox, Pip the squirrel, and Sage the turtle — solve problems the only way anything ever gets solved here: together, with the right creature, in the right order, one careful step at a time.

## The World of Whisperwood

**Songbirds**
The news network of Whisperwood. Fast and wide-ranging. When two relay chains conflict, the network becomes loud and confused. Songbirds transmit — they do not verify.
*Rule: What travels fast does not always travel whole.*

**Raccoons**
The fact-checkers of Whisperwood. They investigate, verify, and keep records. When two accounts conflict, they find the third account — the one nobody thought to look for.
*Rule: A complete story has more than one witness.*
